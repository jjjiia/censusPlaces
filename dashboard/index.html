Using census data to provide a self assessment of exposure to the city.

There is a lot to be gained by using urban data to highlight the ways we use the city, an assessment of our geospatial diet. Specifically, that self assessment of everyday exposure to the city as understood through data has the ability to inform our experiences, provide contextual understanding, highlight limitations in our experiences, and even give us opportunities to balance our diets.

What is the geospatial health of your experiences in a city? in different cities? Do you go to the same type of neighborhoods in Boston, New York, and Seattle? I do.

Through publicly available data of demographics, amenities, and other quality of life indicators, I am measuring the qualities, diversity, and inequality of places that make up my daily routine. I would like to use self assessment as a starting point to better understand the dynamics of my geospatial decisions and challenge the limitations of the often homogeneous places I frequent.

@censusPlaces
This twitter account currently does 2 things:

1. It follows me through the city. Each time I move from one census block to another, the demographics of the place I had just left is tweeted with the amount of time I spent there.

2. It answers questions about your current location with census data. If you tweet at @censusPlaces with any message and your coordinates enabled on the tweet, you will get a reply with data about where you are.

Click on then enable toggle at bottom of screen:


This enables the bot to look up your coordinates, but only for this tweet. Precise location is by default off when location is enabled.

Currently, only data for New York state and Massachusetts are loaded because they are my home states.
